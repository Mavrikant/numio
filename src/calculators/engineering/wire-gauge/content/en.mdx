# Wire Gauge / Ampacity Calculator (AWG)

## What is AWG and Wire Ampacity?

The American Wire Gauge (AWG) system is the standard wire sizing specification used in the United States, Canada, and several other countries. AWG numbers follow a counterintuitive convention: the larger the AWG number, the smaller (thinner) the wire. AWG 22 is a fine wire used for low-power signal circuits, while AWG 4/0 is a heavy-duty cable capable of carrying hundreds of amperes.

**Ampacity** — the portmanteau of "ampere capacity" — is the maximum continuous electrical current that a conductor can carry without exceeding its safe operating temperature. Exceeding the ampacity causes the insulation to overheat, degrade, and potentially start a fire. The ampacity values in this calculator are drawn from NEC Table 310.15(B)(16), which covers copper conductors in free air at 60°C.

The fundamental relationships for a round copper conductor are:

**Diameter (mm):** d = 0.127 × 92^((36 − AWG) / 39)

**Cross-sectional area (mm²):** A = π × (d/2)²

**DC resistance per meter:** R/m = ρ / A, where ρ (copper) = 1.724 × 10⁻⁸ Ω·m at 20°C

**Voltage drop (round trip):** V_drop = I × (R/m) × 2 × L

A voltage drop of more than 3% on a branch circuit is considered excessive by the NEC. For a 120V system, 3% equals 3.6V — beyond this, equipment efficiency decreases and motor life is shortened.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Select Wire Gauge (AWG)**: Choose from 22 AWG (finest) down to 4/0 AWG (thickest). For residential wiring, 14 AWG and 12 AWG are the most common.

2. **Enter Circuit Current (A)**: The current your circuit is designed to carry. This is used for voltage drop calculation.

3. **Enter One-Way Length (m)**: The distance from the power source to the load in meters. The calculator automatically uses twice this distance for the round-trip voltage drop.

4. **Enter Load Current (A)** *(optional)*: If your actual load current differs from the circuit current, enter it here for the suitability check. The wire is suitable when load current does not exceed ampacity.

5. **Read the Results**: You will see the conductor diameter, cross-sectional area, resistance per meter, ampacity, voltage drop, and a suitability verdict.

## Examples

### Example 1 — Residential 20A Kitchen Circuit

A 20A kitchen circuit (AWG 12) runs 15 meters to the far end of the kitchen.

- AWG 12 diameter ≈ 2.053mm, area ≈ 3.309mm²
- Ampacity: **20A** (matches the circuit breaker)
- R/m ≈ 0.00521 Ω/m
- Voltage drop = 20 × 0.00521 × 2 × 15 = **3.13V** (at 120V = 2.6% — within NEC limit)
- Verdict: **Suitable**

### Example 2 — Long Run to Outbuilding

A 60A subpanel feed (AWG 6) runs 60 meters to a detached garage.

- AWG 6 diameter ≈ 4.115mm, area ≈ 13.30mm²
- Ampacity: **55A** ← not sufficient for a 60A load
- Voltage drop = 55 × 0.00130 × 2 × 60 = **8.57V** (at 120V = 7.1% — exceeds limit)
- Verdict: **Not suitable** — upgrade to AWG 4 or use a larger gauge

### Example 3 — Low-Voltage LED Strip

AWG 18 wire powers a 5A LED strip 10 meters away (12V system).

- AWG 18 diameter ≈ 1.024mm, area ≈ 0.823mm²
- Ampacity: **7A** (sufficient for 5A load)
- R/m ≈ 0.02094 Ω/m
- Voltage drop = 5 × 0.02094 × 2 × 10 = **2.09V** (at 12V = 17.4% — critically high!)
- Verdict: **Not suitable** — use AWG 14 or shorten the run

## FAQ

### What does the NEC say about wire sizing?
The National Electrical Code (NFPA 70) requires that conductors be sized for the load they serve, with ampacity equal to or greater than the non-continuous load plus 125% of the continuous load. NEC 310.15(B)(16) provides the base ampacity table for conductors in free air.

### Why does AWG numbering seem backwards?
AWG is historically based on the number of drawing steps needed to produce a wire of that size. More draws = smaller diameter = larger AWG number. The system predates modern engineering notation, hence the inverse relationship.

### What is the difference between 60°C and 75°C ampacity?
The NEC provides separate ampacity columns for 60°C, 75°C, and 90°C rated insulation. Higher-temperature insulation allows more current for the same conductor size. However, most residential wiring devices and terminations are only rated for 60°C or 75°C, so the lower column must be used in practice.

### Should I derate for bundled cables?
Yes. When multiple current-carrying conductors are bundled together in a conduit, the heat cannot dissipate as freely. NEC Article 310 requires derating: 3–5 conductors = 80% of table ampacity, 6–8 conductors = 70%, etc. This calculator assumes a single conductor in free air.

### What is the maximum recommended voltage drop?
NEC recommends: branch circuits (lights, outlets) ≤ 3%; combined feeder + branch ≤ 5%. Some codes and jurisdictions have stricter requirements.
