# Percentage Calculator

The percentage calculator helps you solve the three most common percentage problems in one place: finding a percentage of a number, determining what percentage one number is of another, and calculating the percentage change between two values. Whether you're working on a school assignment, figuring out a discount at the store, or analysing business metrics, this tool gives you instant, accurate answers.

## What Is a Percentage?

A percentage is a way of expressing a number as a fraction of 100. The word comes from the Latin *per centum*, meaning "by the hundred." When we say 25%, we mean 25 out of every 100 — or one quarter of the whole. Percentages are the universal language of comparison: they let us describe proportions, rates, and changes in a consistent way regardless of the scale of the underlying numbers.

Percentages appear throughout everyday life: a 20% tip on a restaurant bill, a 15% VAT tax, a 5% interest rate on a loan, a 30% discount on a product, an 8% annual salary increase, and a 2.5% inflation rate.

## How to Use This Calculator

Select the type of problem you want to solve:

**1. What is X% of Y?**
Enter the percentage (X) and the base number (Y). The result is the portion of Y that X% represents.
*Example: What is 15% of 80? → 12*

**2. X is what percent of Y?**
Enter the two numbers. The result tells you what fraction of Y the number X represents, expressed as a percentage.
*Example: 30 is what percent of 120? → 25%*

**3. Percentage change from X to Y**
Enter the starting value (X) and the ending value (Y). The result is the percentage increase or decrease.
*Example: Change from 50 to 75 → +50% increase*

## Formulas

| Problem Type | Formula |
|---|---|
| X% of Y | Result = (X ÷ 100) × Y |
| X is what % of Y | Result = (X ÷ Y) × 100 |
| Percentage change from X to Y | Result = ((Y − X) ÷ X) × 100 |

## Examples

**Example 1 — Retail discount:**
A jacket originally priced at £120 is on sale at 25% off. What is the discount amount?
→ 25% of 120 = (25 ÷ 100) × 120 = **£30 discount** (sale price: £90)

**Example 2 — Test score:**
A student answered 42 out of 56 questions correctly. What percentage did they get?
→ 42 is what % of 56 = (42 ÷ 56) × 100 = **75%**

**Example 3 — Salary increase:**
An employee's salary increased from $48,000 to $52,800. What is the percentage increase?
→ Percentage change = ((52,800 − 48,000) ÷ 48,000) × 100 = **10% increase**

**Example 4 — Market decline:**
A stock fell from $200 to $170. What is the percentage drop?
→ Percentage change = ((170 − 200) ÷ 200) × 100 = **−15% decrease**

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is the difference between percentage and percentage points?**
Percentage and percentage points are easily confused. If an interest rate rises from 4% to 6%, it has increased by 2 *percentage points*, but the *percentage* increase is 50% (since 2 is 50% of 4). This distinction matters greatly in finance and economics.

**Can a percentage be greater than 100%?**
Yes. If a quantity doubles, it has increased by 100%. If it triples, that's a 200% increase. Percentages above 100% are perfectly valid when representing growth or ratios larger than the base.

**How do I calculate a percentage in my head?**
A useful trick: 10% of any number is simply the number divided by 10. Then you can scale: 5% is half of 10%, 20% is double 10%, 1% is one-tenth of 10%, and so on.

**Why does my calculator give a different answer for percentage change?**
Make sure you are dividing by the *original* value, not the new value. The formula is (new − old) ÷ old × 100, not (new − old) ÷ new × 100.

**What is a percentage of a percentage?**
Multiplying percentages requires care. If you take 20% of 50%, you get 0.20 × 0.50 = 0.10, which is 10%. In everyday language, "20% of 50%" = 10%, not 70%.
