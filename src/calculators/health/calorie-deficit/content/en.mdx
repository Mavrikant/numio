## What Is a Calorie Deficit?

A calorie deficit occurs when you consume fewer calories than your body burns. Your body then draws on stored energy — primarily body fat — to make up the difference. Over time, this creates measurable weight loss.

**No deficit = no weight loss.** Maintenance calories keep your weight stable.

## The 7,700 kcal/kg Rule

This calculator uses the well-established clinical rule:

**1 kg of body fat ≈ 7,700 kilocalories of stored energy**

To lose 1 kg, you must create a total calorie deficit of approximately **7,700 kcal**. This is why:

- A deficit of 500 kcal/day → 3,500 kcal/week → ~0.45 kg/week
- A deficit of 1,000 kcal/day → 7,000 kcal/week → ~0.9 kg/week

## How the Calculator Works

1. **Weight to lose**: current weight − target weight (converted to kg if imperial)
2. **Total calories needed**: weight to lose (kg) × 7,700
3. **Weekly deficit**: daily deficit × 7
4. **Weeks to goal**: total calories ÷ weekly deficit (rounded up)
5. **Months to goal**: weeks ÷ 4.33

## Why Weight Loss Is Not Linear

The calculator provides a best-case estimate. Real-world weight loss is rarely a straight line because:

1. **Metabolic adaptation**: As you lose weight, your body needs fewer calories. Your basal metabolic rate decreases with mass, requiring you to re-evaluate your deficit.
2. **Water retention**: Dietary changes, hormonal shifts, salt intake, and exercise all cause water retention to fluctuate — masking fat loss on the scale.
3. **Plateaus**: After weeks of consistent deficit, the body adapts and weight loss slows or temporarily stops.
4. **Muscle vs. fat**: A significant deficit without adequate protein and resistance training leads to muscle loss, which reduces the calorie-burning value of each kg lost.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Why not just eat as little as possible?**
Very low-calorie diets (below 800 kcal/day) cause muscle loss, nutrient deficiencies, hormonal disruption, and severe metabolic adaptation. Most of the "quick" weight lost is water and muscle — and weight typically rebounds quickly.

**How do I know my daily deficit?**
Estimate your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) using a BMR calculator and an activity multiplier. Then subtract your actual calorie intake. The gap is your deficit.

**Should I also exercise?**
Exercise increases your TDEE, allowing a larger effective deficit without needing to cut more food. It also preserves muscle mass, improves insulin sensitivity, and has numerous health benefits beyond weight loss.

**What is the 7,700 number based on?**
The energy density of adipose (fat) tissue is approximately 9 kcal per gram of fat. Since body fat is not 100% pure triglycerides (it contains water and protein), the effective value per kg of body weight lost is approximately 7,700 kcal — a clinically established average used in dietetics.

**Why doesn't the calculator show a target date?**
A date implies precision that doesn't exist. Weight loss is non-linear. Showing a date like "June 15" would be misleading when your actual timeline depends on daily compliance, metabolic adaptation, and biological variability.
