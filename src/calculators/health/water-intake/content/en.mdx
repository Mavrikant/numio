# Daily Water Intake Calculator

Proper hydration is one of the simplest and most powerful things you can do for your health. This calculator takes your body weight, physical activity level, climate, and reproductive status to give you a personalised daily water target backed by scientific guidelines.

## What is Daily Water Intake?

Daily water intake refers to the total amount of fluid your body needs each day to maintain normal physiological functions — regulating body temperature, transporting nutrients, flushing waste products, lubricating joints, and supporting digestion.

The most widely cited evidence-based formula uses **35 ml of water per kilogram of body weight** as the baseline. This figure is derived from the Dietary Reference Intakes established by the National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine (NASEM). Individual requirements then increase based on:

- **Activity level**: Physical exercise causes sweating and increases respiratory water loss. Light activity adds approximately 350 ml/day, while very intense daily exercise or a physically demanding job can require 1,400 ml/day above the baseline.
- **Climate**: In hot environments, the body sweats more to regulate temperature. A hot climate adds ~350 ml/day; extreme heat (deserts, tropical settings) may require an additional 700 ml/day.
- **Pregnancy**: The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists recommends an additional 300 ml/day during pregnancy to support amniotic fluid and foetal development.
- **Breastfeeding**: Producing breast milk demands an additional 700 ml/day to maintain maternal hydration and milk supply.

## How to Use This Calculator

1. **Enter your body weight** in kilograms. Use your current weight, ideally measured in the morning before eating.
2. **Select your activity level** — choose from sedentary (desk job, no exercise) to very active (daily intense training or physical labour).
3. **Choose your climate** — the environment where you spend most of your waking hours.
4. **Toggle pregnancy or breastfeeding** if applicable.
5. **Read your results** — the calculator shows your total daily water need in millilitres, litres, fluid ounces, and the equivalent number of 250 ml glasses.

The results represent total fluid intake from all beverages. About 20% of daily water needs are typically met through food, so your actual drinking target may be slightly lower if your diet includes plenty of fruits, vegetables, soups, and other hydrating foods.

## Examples

**Example 1 — Office worker in a temperate climate:**
A 70 kg person with a sedentary desk job living in a mild climate needs a baseline of 70 × 35 = 2,450 ml/day. With no activity or climate bonus, the recommended intake is **2,450 ml (2.45 litres)**, or about 10 glasses of 250 ml.

**Example 2 — Recreational runner in a hot climate:**
A 75 kg person who runs 5 days per week (moderate activity) in a hot, humid environment: 75 × 35 = 2,625 ml base + 700 ml (moderate activity) + 350 ml (hot climate) = **3,675 ml (3.68 litres)** per day, or about 15 glasses.

**Example 3 — Breastfeeding mother:**
A 65 kg mother who breastfeeds and lives a light-activity lifestyle in a temperate climate: 65 × 35 = 2,275 ml + 350 ml (light activity) + 700 ml (breastfeeding) = **3,325 ml (3.33 litres)** per day.

## Understanding Your Results

Your calculator output shows five values:
- **Millilitres (ml)** — the base scientific unit, most precise
- **Litres (L)** — convenient for grocery shopping (water bottles are labelled in litres)
- **Fluid ounces (fl oz)** — useful if you follow US-standard measurements
- **Cups (240 ml)** — a common kitchen measure for tracking intake
- **Glasses (250 ml)** — a practical daily counting target; displayed as a rounded-up whole number

Aim to spread your intake throughout the day rather than drinking large amounts at once. Starting with a glass upon waking and sipping consistently before feeling thirsty are evidence-backed habits for maintaining good hydration.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Does coffee or tea count toward daily fluid intake?**
Yes. Caffeinated beverages contribute to hydration. Early research suggested caffeine was strongly diuretic, but more recent studies show that moderate consumption (3–4 cups/day) does not cause net fluid loss. However, water and herbal teas remain the optimal choices because they provide hydration without caffeine tolerance effects.

**Is the popular "8 glasses a day" rule accurate?**
The "8×8" rule (eight 8-ounce glasses, approximately 1.9 litres) is a useful minimum floor for many adults, but it does not account for body size, physical activity, or climate. A 100 kg manual labourer in a desert needs far more than a 50 kg sedentary office worker in a cool climate. The weight-based formula used here provides a significantly more personalised estimate.

**What happens if I drink too much water?**
Overhydration (hyponatraemia) is rare but can occur when sodium levels in the blood become dangerously diluted, typically in endurance athletes who drink excessive plain water over many hours without replacing electrolytes. For most healthy adults, the kidneys can process about 800–1,000 ml of water per hour, making overhydration from normal drinking habits very unlikely.

**How can I tell if I am drinking enough?**
The simplest self-check is urine colour: pale yellow (straw-coloured) indicates good hydration; dark yellow or amber suggests dehydration; colourless may indicate overhydration. Other signs of adequate hydration include moist lips, no persistent headache, and stable energy levels.

**Should I drink more when ill?**
Yes. Fever, vomiting, and diarrhoea all increase fluid and electrolyte losses significantly. Oral rehydration solutions (ORS) are preferable during illness to replace both fluid and electrolytes. Consult a healthcare professional if symptoms are severe or prolonged.
